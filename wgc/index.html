The Geologic Time Scale

Extremely broad strokes

The planet has existed for 4.54 billion years. To better understand the phases in such a massive time scale, we need Geology.

This is the common time scale of an year:

year > month > week > day > hour > minute

And this is the geologic time scale:

Eon > Era > Period > Epoch > Age > Chron

There are four eons, but it’s both common and useful to group the first three together into one supereon. As a result, we’re left with the Precambrian supereon and the Phanerozoic eon. The cutting point between the two is the Cambrian Explosion, when life on the planet suddenly exploded and became more abundant. This happened in the Phanerozoic Eon, Paleozoic Era, Cambrian Period.

After the Paleozoic Era, comes the Mesozoic Era (the first of the Phanerozoic Eon). This is the Era of the dinosaurs, with three Periods: Triassic, Jurassic and Cretaceous. The change from the Mesozoic Era of the Cretaceous Period to the Cenozoic Era of the Paleogene Period is quite familiar to most people: it’s the Cretaceous-Paleogene Extinction Event, which happened 66 million years ago. That’s the meteor that killed the dinosaurs.

Now let’s backtrack a little bit before we come back to the meteor.

Very broad strokes

Here’s a very broad strokes understanding of the geologic history of the planet so far:

  1. Precambrian Supereon (origin of life)
  2. Phanerozoic Eon
  1. Paleozoic Era (cambrian explosion)
  2. Mesozoic Era (dinosaurs)
  3. Cenozoic Era (mammals)

Broad strokes

Now, with a little bit of more detail:

  1. The Earth was formed 4.6 billions years ago. This is the start of the Precambrian Supereon, that is, everything before the Cambrian Explosion.
  2. 500 million years ago, the Cambrian Explosion happens. The planet had bacteria and other microorganisms then, but since the Cambrian Explosion we have the first fossils, the first signs of animal life.
  3. With the Cambrian Explosion, the Precambrian Supereon is over and we get into the Phanerozoic Eon. Since we are still in the Phanerozoic Eon, that’s it for eons.
  4. As the Cambrian Explosion happens, we get into the Paleozoic Era, Cambrian Period. You may think of the entire Paleozoic Era as the building up of living organisms on the Planet, from microorganisms to dinosaurs.
  5. After the Paleozoic Era, we have the Mesozoic Era. You can think of this as the Era of dinosaurs.
  6. Once the dinosaurs are extinct, their era is over and the mammals take over. We move on to the Cenozoic Era and this is where we are today.

The extinction of the Dinosaurs

And now we circle back to the meteor that killed the dinosaurs.

Most people know the basics about the Cretaceous-Paleogene Extinction Event: a large meteor hit the planet and all the dinosaurs died. Here’s some additional information about it.

  1. It happened 66 million years ago.
  2. The impact happened at the Yucatán Peninsula, in Mexico, near the Caribbean, leaving the Chicxulub crater. It’s force is about a billion times that of the atomic bombs used on Japan at the end of World War 2.
  3. As a result of the impact, the atmosphere of the planet caught on fire for a brief time, incinerating the surface. Longer-lasting firestorms formed on the planet after this, causing further destruction. Signs of this can be found on the planet today.
  4. Three of every four species of plants and animals on the planet became extinct.
  5. The extinction of the dinosaurs directly led to the rise of mammals, because only small animals that burrow underground were able to survive. Mammal species grew 10% in size in the following millions of years.
  6. The meteor impact also caused longer-lasting global climate change, a so-called “impact winter”, a rise of the sea level and the occurrence of mega tsunamis that caused further destruction.

After the meteor, we get into the Cenozoic Era. This is when the continents assume the shapes we know today.

This is not a children’s book, much less a guide on being a good slave, so we now proceed to understand the basic notions of Geology.

Geology is one of the noblest sciences, the study of this planet and, after recent developments, of other celestial bodies as well. A good definition of Geology is always wide enough that it will make you believe every geologist to be megalomaniac, concerned with the affairs of a dozen lesser sciences. They will study the highest layer of the atmosphere, the deepest core of the planet and everything in between. The moment the first man considered a type of stone to be more proper for something than another type, he was the first geologist. That was certainly essential knowledge, as Geology still is very much essential today. This is important to distinguish it from sciences that have lost its importance (such as Geography), that have never been important (such as Sociology and Anthropology) or that have prostituted themselves to power and weakness (such as Medicine, Psychology and Philosophy).

All great civilizations of the past have studied the planet, but the earliest works known come from Ancient Greece, by a man called Theophrastus, the successor of Aristotle as head of the Lyceum, his Philosophy school. It is certain that Aristotle himself had written on the subject, though most of his works have been lost. For example, Aristotle wrote about fossils of seashells found in rocks and inferred that the positions of land and sea had changed and that these changes had happened slowly over a long period of time. Another Ancient Greek, Eratosthenes, was capable of calculating the circumference of the Earth with remarkable accuracy.

Nicolas Steno (1638-1686), a Danish and one of the founders of modern Geology, is credited with three important principles of Geology, that is, basic facts about the planet that can be used as guides to understand direct experience and expand knowledge: the law of superposition, the principle of original horizontality and the principle of lateral continuity. As a group, they mean that older material is found under new material, deposited horizontally in all directions until a barrier is met. This is how many fossils are dated by archeologists: they know when the soil around the fossil was deposited. Steno was a true scientist, having contributed to knowledge of Anatomy and, in a manner related to is studies in Geology, of Paleontology, Stratigraphy and Crystallography, the study of crystals, where he is credited for the First Law of Crystallography, which states that the angles between the faces of crystals and solids are constant for every example of the same type. Raised as a Lutheran, he converted to Catholicism and later became a Catholic Bishop.

James Hutton (1726-1797), a Scottish, considered the first modern geologist, is credited with another principle of geology, Uniformitarianism, which states that the past experienced the same natural laws we experience today. At the time, it meant to reject explanations based on catastrophes, that is, unique and powerful events. Though now we know that such events can occur (such as the extinction of the dinosaurs), it is still valid for the larger part of geological features. He also proposed, correctly, that the interior of the Earth was hot and the engine of creation of new rock, expelled by volcanoes.

In 1850, Sir Charles Lyell (1797-1875), a British, published his Principles of Geology. This widely important book influenced Charles Darwin and helped establish the basis of the science.

As technology and general scientific knowledge grew, Geology continued useful and illuminating. New technologies allowed progressively better estimations of the planet’s age. The knowledge, since the 19th century, that the Earth might be several billion years old, enlarged its explanatory scope enough that the theory of plate tectonics could be developed in the 20th century: the continents themselves move across the planet, extremely slowly, but over billions of years.

Through the study of Geology, you will understand how geographical features around you came to be and what the types of rock are. It enables you to, for example, find out how likely it is that there is oil underneath the ground, and how deep.

The evolutionary history of the Homo sapiens sapiens

The evolutionary history of humans starts 7 million years ago, so 59 million years into the Cenozoic Era, in Africa.

First we have the sahelanthropus, for 3 million years, then the australopithecus, for 2 million years. Both lived in Africa and are not considered humans.

The “Anatomically Modern Human” shows up around 200,000 years ago. That’s the Homo sapiens sapiens, our species.

There were other species of the Homo genus, also not considered humans. They have all since become extinct. Every several years archeologists will discover the remains of another one.

Once the different groups of Homo sapiens sapiens were scattered on the planet, they evolved separately into the racial differences we see today.

Neanderthals

After leaving Africa 200,000 years ago, early Home sapiens sapiens interbred with Neanderthals, which had left between 200,000 and 600,000 years before, having evolved separately during this time to adapt to the radically different European climate. As a result, the DNA of all non-African races consists between 1% and 4% of Neanderthal DNA. Around 30,000 years ago, Neanderthals were either extinct or simply dissolved by interbreeding with humans.

Homo sapiens sapiens

All those Homo sapiens sapiens lived as nomadic hunter-gatherers all over the world. Their diet consisted mostly of scavenged vegetables—fruits, berries, nuts—and meat from hunting. It was a difficult and dangerous life, as people still did not have dominion over nature. The most important advancement from then on was the accumulation of knowledge. It allowed them to outperform every other predator and every other subspecies of human evolution. This upgrade came not from further evolution, but by the accumulation of experience and knowledge. With the development of Agriculture, societies became more complex and thus more powerful. Man becomes the wolf of man.

At around 11,000 b.C, the end of the last Ice Age, humans start to live in villages. Within a few thousand years, plant and animal domestication begins. By then humans had reached the Americas. They were still to reach Madagascar and New Zealand—the large islands next to Africa and Australia, respectively.

Africa has so many large mammals today because they evolved together with humans, learning to fear them. By contrast, both in the Americas and in Australia, the arrival of humans meant the doom of native large mammals. For example, Australia had giant kangaroos and the Americas had giant ground sloths and mammoths. All were consumed by men, which they were not prepared to react against.

Wherever humans reached, they spread and multiplied fast. In a thousand years they populated the Americas.

Mountains and volcanoes

A volcano is a type of mountain, as in "large and tall landforms", and both result from tectonic plate movement. Non-volcanic mountains are elevated masses of rock. Most are part of mountain chains, large elevated areas between tectonic plates.

Tectonic plate movement is the most powerful force shaping the planet. Where tectonic plates clash, either one or both of them climb, expelling underground material. If the tectonic plates move away from each other, leaving the underground unexposed, the same happens. The volcano is the conduit of this exposure. One volcanic eruption may expel magma, volcanic ash, gases or combinations of them.

In the year 62, there was a powerful earthquake in the Italian Peninsula, around the Apennines mountain chain, in the western shore of the Italian Peninsula. Another happened in 64, while the Roman Emperor Nero performed in a public theater. He kept singing through the earthquake. The theater collapsed soon after it was evacuated. The people of the region were accustomed to them. Other earthquakes happened four four days in 79, but were similarly not cause for great concern. On the fifth day, Mount Vesuvius, part of the Apennines, started erupting, lasting for two days. It was one of the most devastating volcanic eruptions in history, equating a hundred thousand times the heat of the Hiroshima atomic bomb. Herculaneum and Pompeii took the brunt of the damage and were completely destroyed, an archeological treasure for the modern times. The ash covered the cities, burning people alive or suffocating them with ash.

Hills

Under the lake on the comic, you will find a few hills. There is no universal height when a hill becomes a mountain. In practice, however, it is easy enough to spot the difference, because they are shorter and less steep. Hills may come from tectonic plate movement, but more often it's by accumulation from erosion. Rains, rivers, glaciers or wind can be the ultimate cause.

Mesas

There is one last elevated formation on the comic: mesas. The word means “table”, which is descriptive enough. They originate from erosion of the top part of hills and mountains.

Lakes

Now notice the lake on the right side of the volcano on the map. That might be coincidence, but it makes perfect sense. That might be a crater lake, created when volcanoes cause the ground around them to sink. Once the crater is set, a water source can fill it up with water. In the comic, there are two rivers streaming into the pictured lake. In a similar way, some volcanoes create a lake surrounding them, called “volcanic lakes”. Glaciers

Speaking of glaciers, you can see one in the comic. They are accumulations of ice on top of mountains due to the cold weather on high altitudes. The cold weather is a result of less atmospheric pressure, which leads to less air, meaning less matter to gather heat from the Sun. Glaciers are massive and kept in constant movement due to the weight of ever accumulating snow. They partially melt in the warm months, giving rise to rivers that increase discharge in the summer.

Rivers

We move on to rivers. There are two sources for free-flowing surface water: melting glaciers and water springs. Both are present in the comic. Water springs do not have a constant discharge, varying according to rainfall. The rainwater then flows through the ground and becomes trapped in cave systems. Some may dry up completely in the low-rainfall season. Hot water springs also exist, with water pumped from underground by volcanic activity. Below you will see an example of a water spring.

A drainage basin or catchment area is the area drained by a river. All humidity in that area flows into the river, be it originated from rainfall, tributary rivers or glaciers. In the comic, that would be the mountain range, as well as the area surrounding the tributary rivers and the main river itself. The forest area is not part of the drainage basin, as it seems to feed on rainfall from the Ocean humidity. This same humidity stops at the mountain range, giving origin to the desert. Deltas and Estuaries

Upon reaching the ocean, a river might form a delta or an estuary. Though you will find those terms used without discretion as if interchangeable, there is a difference. Deltas are triangular shapes, as the river spreads on many smaller streams closer to the coast. They are fertile, because this river formation distributes its nutrient-rich sediment around. In estuaries, by contrast, rivers reach the ocean with more momentum. They drop straight into the ocean, affecting the landscape to a lesser degree.

Biomes

Biomes are areas with similar conditions of climate, fauna and flora. A more popularized name for biome would be “ecosystem”. The whole planet is an ecosystem, as in a balanced ecological system, but regions may have a separate balance of their own. The interdependence between different elements may be surprising. Some trees are essential to their whole ecosystem, meanwhile being dependent on certain animals. At the same time, all are dependent on the climate and on the water cycle. Inside a biome you may have another biome of relative isolation, such as an oasis in a desert, or on mountains. The world climate is in constant change, both from natural and from man-made causes. On Geography

Geography, meaning “description of the Earth”, has been much degraded as a science in our time. The act of drawing maps has completely moved over to Cartography, more a skill than a science. The most technical and in depth description of the Earth has been undertaken by Geology, which is a technical science. The study of the superficial configuration of land falls to Geomorphology, which is essentially part of Geography, and also a technical science, but clearly subservient to Geology as well. The unique part of Geography today would be Human Geography, which has described itself in the 20th century as the study of “space”, a concept meaning the configuration of human presence on the face of the Earth. Though this is a valid area of study, geographers have also here fallen behind other sciences, and the origin of this might be traced back to a fundamental mistake in the concept of space itself or perhaps, in how Geography will go about studying it.

For example, there is Urban Geography. However, the more useful and interesting share of the study of urban development falls under Urbanism, which has become closer to Architecture, a more practical area of study. No matter what principles or decades of study Geography does of urban space, the fact that Geographers won’t be called upon to act on the development of cities makes them powerless and ineffectual. An urbanist’s or architect’s notion of the possibilities of urban arrangement will be much wider, because he is trained to conceive of new possibilities, and more practical, since he is expected to create plans that can be made into reality.

The same goes for Rural Geography, which is in a particularly sad state, as Geographers, confused with the infusion of technologies into rural space, stupidly declare that there is no more rural space, as it has turned into urban space. This is a complete failure in the understanding of knowledge itself and a blunder that can’t possibly be excused. This is of course of little importance, since geographers’ misconceptions have little, if any, effect on our lives.

And finally, Cultural Geography and Economic Geography are mainly vehicles of propaganda for ideologies originated from even less important sciences, such as Sociology and Anthropology. Geographers that do not adhere to these ideologies have tried to create meaningful and useful uses for their science without adhering to propaganda, but they have fallen short of being useful. As it turns out, you will understand your space better by reading the opinions of classified people with experience of the real world, be they geographers or not. The study of works of academic Geography itself are useless to build this understanding. In contrast, one can’t possibly talk about their country’s economy intelligently without being updated with the studies of Economics.

The aforementioned Jared Diamond, for example, resorts much more to other sciences to achieve his conclusions. Even if you concede that, perhaps, being a geographer means just that: studying many different disciplines and synthesizing them, the truth is that any intelligent person so disposed can achieve this without ever having read a book produced by academic geographers. There are no principles of Geography to be considered, no laws, no intellectual tools to better clarify our understanding of our surroundings. The concept of space itself is useless for this.

Something similar happens with military geography, which is today, as it has always been, important in devising military strategy. Again, academic geographical studies are useless for this, as military study and experience is much more important. Knowledge of geographic features and their meaning is thus important, but not knowledge of Geography.

As Geography slips into irrelevance in all these things, it also mocks and discards the works of 19th century geographers such as Karl Ritter and Paul Vidal de la Blache, regarded as mere justification for nationalistic political ideologies—that is, according to modern geographers also intended in using the science for the propaganda of their own ideologies. Both Ritter and Vidal de la Blache were important intellectuals of their time and deserve respect as intelligent men looking for the truth, as opposed to vectors of political interests of their nations. Even so, something is off about trying to continue in their tradition. The sort of ideas they tackled simply do not belong in Geography or can be trusted to geographers alone, just as no one consults sociologists on the state of our society or how to shape it—an immoral project of the highest order, in my opinion.

Most of all, 19th century Geography was the home of one of the greatest thinkers of the time, Alexander von Humboldt (1769-1859), a German. The ignorance of his work by geographers is pathetic, but it hardly matters, as his contribution to human understanding goes beyond the discipline. He is simply one of the most important men of his time, who will always be remembered by history.

And yet, as much as academic Geography is useless, the study of Geography in school is important for children and teenagers. We were all brought kicking and screaming into globalization, and now we face a reality where everything is connected and interdependent. Free trade has always been a good thing throughout human history, as it brings possibilities for mutually beneficial exchanges.

Most human right are a lie, a luxury of these times of abundance, but of the few real human rights there are, free trade is one of the most important. By free trade alone, you can raise yourself from nothing to the highest echelons of society, if only you have the intelligence, as many have had. That doesn’t have to be your aim individually, of course, the accumulation of wealth and power does not have to be the goal of every person. The knowledge that it is possible, however, is essential. If a person learns anything from school level Geography, it should be that there is a world beyond their street, their state, their country. There are languages to learn, people to meet and opportunities to take advantage of all over the world. The future will not be built, as the present wasn’t, by people who limit their thinking to their immediate surroundings, especially not today. As long as there are people who think of the world a whole, and calibrate their ambitions accordingly, you will be in a disadvantage if you don’t have the same forward thinking.

On geographical features

I once read an article speculating on the evolutionary origin of the sense of beauty. Images of human bodies, both male and female, of babies and children and of food have all been historically considered pleasing to look at and have been subject of countless paintings. The evolutionary meaning of these things are easy to surmise: reproduction, survival. Images of heroic deeds and battles also can be seen to have the same effect of Epic literature, to teach by example and to inspire. Abstract art, such as geometric patterns in muslim mosques, appeals to our love of order and logical arrangement, as well as Architecture. It pleases us to see a building that is solid, logical, graceful and powerful, perhaps we would like to build ourselves to be thus, or to take pleasure from the results of hard work. A painting of a boat can appeal to our wanderlust, our sense of the endless possibility of human experience. Paintings of deformed beings, disease, horror and rotten still life lead to the consideration of death and suffering, two facts of life, and the avoidance of thought in these things is a proof positive of a foolish mind, as they can’t be avoided in reality and it is wise to prepare for what is known to be coming.

All these are useful instincts for a man to have, but one main type of beauty is missing: landscapes. One theory the article mentioned, which convinced me, is that it is reassuring to be able to see far ahead. If you’re alone in the wild, you will desire at least to be at an advantage point, so you will know of threats in advance. In effect, many cities have been built on high ground, surrounding mountaintop castles. Coastal cities gaze not inward, but to the sea, where coming threats can be spotted from far away, and this view is frequently the most desired.

At the same time, if you’re on higher ground, any attacking force is at a disadvantage. The enemy will arrive tired, you will be rested. Mongol armies used this technique when attacking Europe. They would flee from the European knights to the high ground, where they had previously left an additional supply of horses. As the Europeans reach them, their horses tired, the Mongols had rested horses to counter attack with.

The Caspian sea is the final destination of the water carried by the Volga river, the most important river of Russia. To drive home how exceptional this is, remember that most other endorheic basins are deserts. Of the endorheic basins that do have rivers and lakes, the Volga/Caspian is the largest. The main biomes

A biome or ecosystem is an area of similar climatic conditions and similar presence of life. The original life forms in a biome either evolved together or merged together at some moment. Excluding human influence, there is ecological balance between life population, geographical formations and climate. Introduced species may disturb this balance, but not always. In a similar way, humans may coexist without disturbing the ecosystem. The degree of disturbance varies according to culture and population size.

Biomes follow from the climate found in the region. Below you will find a graph showing the biomes formed according to annual rainfall and average temperature.

The Holdridge life zones system is another scheme to visualize biomes, more detailed than the graph above. It is quite more complex, but it pays off to take a minute to understand its meaning.

Below you will find an extraordinary vegetation world map from Wikipedia. We will use it as a guide and go through each biome from top to bottom.

Polar Desert

Polar deserts have low precipitation and a mean temperature, during summer, of less than 10°C. We find polar deserts near Earth’s North and South Pole. Antarctica is not included in the Vegetation Map above, but most of its area would fall under this classification.

Tundra

Tundra forms in areas where the extreme cold and short summers do not allow for tree growth. Unlike in polar deserts, in Tundras you will find small plants such as shrub, grass and moss, as well as lichen. This stark environment does not allow an abundance of animal life, but birds do migrate to tundras for water. There are three types of tundra: Arctic, Alpine and Antarctic. Alpine Tundra forms where high altitudes create an environment akin to the polar environment.

Taiga

As we move further south from the poles, conifers are the first kind of trees we will find. Taigas are the zone marked by their presence alone among trees. Their leaves are long and thin, some are scale-like, and they are not shed before winter. Conservation of energy is key to survive with little sunlight. Conifers’ leaves do not provide enough nutrients for most animals, but taigas do have more animal life than tundras.

Temperate Broadleaf Forest

Moving further south, as days and summers become longer, it is important for trees to be able to capture the extra sunlight. For this, they need broad leaves, unlike the hard, needle-like conifer leaves. But these broad leaves cannot withstand winter. For this reason, deciduous trees suck back the nutrients from the leaves, then shed them. This is why they lose the green color. Animal life becomes richer as leaves are more edible.

Temperate Steppe

Going further south, we find Steppe where there isn’t enough water to sustain trees. In these areas we find grasses and shrubs. Temperature variation is great. Between summer and winter, because of proximity to the poles. Between day and night, because low air humidity doesn’t allow for heat retention.

Subtropical Rainforest

Subtropical rainforests enjoy larger precipitation than the previous biomes. Limitations such as short summers and low sunlight are also absent. This allows for more richness of both plant and animal life. Many of the species found on subtropical rainforests are the same found in tropical rainforests. The difference is that less precipitation and lower temperatures make their presence more sparse.

Mediterranean Vegetation

In the Mediterranean we find scrubby but dense vegetation, broad-leaves shrubs, bushes and small trees. These are characteristic of hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters. Most rainfall happens during winters. There are summer storms, but in general it is subject to many dry months. Mediterranean plant life does not shed leaves before winter - meaning, they are “evergreen”.

Monsoon Forest

Monsoon forests are characteristic of areas subject to monsoon rains, which we will review in a later chapter. These areas have a long dry season followed by a season of heavy rainfall. Some plants shed their leaves during the dry season. We find this biome in Southeast Asia and one typical plant is the bamboo.

Arid Desert

Arid regions have a severe lack of humidity, which in turn causes a lack of nutrients, preventing animal and plant life. Because air humidity is low, there are extreme variations in temperature between night and day. Coupled with the lack of plant life to protect the soil, this results in further degradation of rock and nutrients. Nearby mountain ranges are the ultimate cause for some deserts, as they force clouds upwards. In these cases, rain falls on one side of the mountain chain, but not on the other, which then becomes a desert.

Water presence, such as from a passing river carrying nutrient-rich sediment, eases this condition. Oases are another source of water, resulting from springs and outflows of underground water. The result is vegetation and animal life present in the margins of rivers or oases.

Xeric Shrublands

As we move away from deserts into more moisture-rich regions, plant life begins to return. Xeric Shrublands are this transition zone. It supports tough and small vegetation, shrubs, which are able to survive with low humidity. Some animal life is present, such as reptiles. Xeric Shrublands are fragile and turn desertic with disruption.

Dry Steppe

In a past section we saw Temperate Steppe. The Dry Steppe is similar, but drier, closer to a desert. In practice, it is difficult to distinguish between the two kinds and even between them and Xeric Shrublands. You will find all three referred to simply as steppes, for example.

Semi-Arid Desert

Also referred to as steppe sometimes. Semi-deserts are mild deserts, with less temperature variation and higher humidity. For this reason, the soil is not as degraded as explained before. They form in the transition between deserts and more humid areas.

Grass Savanna

We find Savannas in regions where the soil is able to sustain forests, but the climate is not humid enough. For this reason, they are fluid and expand or contract according to the conditions found. For example, deforestation of the Amazon Forest in Brazil gives place to “cerrado”, a type of savanna. This deforestation might be man-made, but it may also caused by natural climate fluctuation. A strong rainy season might expand the Amazon Forest and contract the cerrado, for example.

Tree Savanna

Tree Savannas are of course alike grass savannas, the difference being the presence of trees. This can follow from soil profile. The afore-mentioned cerrado itself has two types: cerrado stricto sensu and cerrado dense. The latter is closer to a grass savanna, while the second is closer to a tree savanna. Though savannas are more open spaces than trees, the tree numbers might be similar. The difference is, higher humidity in forests allow trees to be larger, wider, and as a result their canopy cover a larger area.

Subtropical Dry Forest

Tropical Dry Forests populated areas where, though having a strong rainy season, also sport long dry seasons. Some tree species will shed leaves before the dry season, if they do not have a nearby source of water. As the trees shed leaves, they allow the sunlight to reach the ground, causing grass to grow. This would not happen in a Tropical Rainforest such as the Amazon, where barely any light reaches the soil.

Tropical Rainforest

Around the Equator, sunlight is abundant and always present. If precipitation is also supplied year-round, a tropical rainforest forms. There is no need for any shedding of leaves, as there is no dry nor cold season. The climate is always hot, always wet. Plant cover is overwhelming, only around 2% of sunlight reaches the soil. See the photo below for an example.

You might not have a correct impression of a rainforest from watching movies. Traversing a rainforest is difficult, as plants stand close to each other. Dead leaves and other plant material, as well as roots, cover the soil. The temperature is high, there is no wind. You can’t see too far ahead because of all the plants blocking your view. To pass through a virgin rainforest, you need to carry some kind of blade and hack through as you go. They cover some of the hardest areas to explore in the planet, reason why they are sparsely populated and largely unknown.

Alpine Tundra

Alpine Tundras form in high altitudes, where it is too cold and winds are too strong to sustain trees. As a result, grass cover is dominant.

Montane Forest

Montane Forests form in moderate elevations, not harsh enough to force an Alpine Tundra.

Most exploration of the planet followed courses of rivers and when not present, human presence is small or absent. In history, rivers have been both pathways of war and natural barriers used in strategy. It’s no wonder many national borders follow the courses of rivers.

The end result is that many major regional divisions of the planet follow from rivers. In turn, Regions are important for understanding the planet and a building block of Geography. The main deserts

Deserts are dry areas due to receiving low amounts of precipitation - be it from rain, snow, mist or fog. There are four classifications of deserts: hot, cold, semi arid and coastal.

The understanding of the function of water in the environment is important to explain hot deserts. As explained in an earlier chapter, water functions as a carrier of temperature in nature. In rainforests, nights tend not to be much colder than days for this reason. In hot deserts, because there is little cloud cover during the day, days are hotter than in others environments. Come night, all this heat dissipates for lack of humidity, and it becomes much colder. This great variation of temperature in one day-cycle further degrades rocks. Because this condition has been present for a long time, it has had lasting effect upon the soil and the presence of life. Animals and plants that can survive with little humidity might be present. A topical presence of water form micro-environments, but it cannot have effect over large areas. This is the case of oases and passing rivers.

Cold deserts are dry because the air is too cold, so there is no evaporation of snow cover. The little precipitation they get fall in the form of snow and accumulates with the snow cover in a similar way to sand. The snow forms dunes and blizzards like sand dunes and sand storms in arid deserts.

Semiarid deserts form in the transition between deserts and other biomes, as the humidity rises. As we have seen in the biomes chapter, there are many different kinds. They may have short rainy seasons that are not enough to change the landscape from a desert to another biome.

Coastal deserts are sometimes long and thin, following along a coast and a mountain range. As explained before, the mountains force clouds upwards, causing them to rain, but only on one side. Besides losing humidity, these air currents also lose heat as they move upwards. For this reason, these deserts are also colder and have lower temperature variation in the day-cycle.

Antarctica

Most of mainland Antarctica is a cold or polar desert. When precipitation occurs, it’s in the form of snow, which accumulates on top of more snow cover. Because snow is white and reflective, it reflects the sunlight, further diminishing heat absorption. Since the snow doesn’t melt or evaporate, the air is also kept dry. Few vegetation other than mosses and fungi are present, and more towards the coast.

Inside the Antarctic Polar Desert we find the snow-free McMurdo Dry Valleys, one of the most extreme deserts on Earth. See a map and a photograph below.

Sahara

The Sahara is the largest hot desert in the world. It borders the Mediterranean biome to the North, the Atlantic Ocean to the West and the Red Sea to the East. South of the Sahara lies the Sahel, a semi-arid transition zone, picture below.

The Arabian Desert

The Arabian Desert covers much of the Arabian Peninsula. At the core of the Arabian Desert lies the Rub’ al Khali, the largest sand desert in the world, pictured below.

The Gobi Desert

The Gobi Desert is the largest desert in Asia. It stretches from Mongolia to Southern China. It is the product of the rain shadow created by the Himalaya mountain range, which blocks rain clouds from the Indian Ocean. This was the land crossed by the Silk Road and ruled by Genghis Khan.

Kalahari Desert

The Kalahari is a semi-arid desert, enjoying a short rainy season. For this reason, it supports more plant and animal life than the deserts mentioned above. It is the theatre of one of the great animal migrations on the planet, featuring zebras, wildebeest, lions, cheetahs, leopards, wild dogs, hyenas and others. They follow the summer rains, when the desert turns into a large green oasis overnight.

Patagonian Desert

The Patagonian Desert, or Patagonian Steppe, borders the Andes mountain chain to the West and the Atlantic Ocean to the East. It is semi-arid in the north, but with a core of shrubby and herbaceous vegetation. It is the product of the rain shadow from the Andes.

Australian Desert

The largest of them is the Great Victorian Desert, which features grasslands, shrublands and salt lakes. Rainfall is irregular but larger than in the previous deserts.

Syrian Desert

The Syrian desert is rocky and flat, lying south of Mesopotamia. In parts it is a steppe, in others, a harsher desert.

The Great Basin Desert is a coastal desert affected by a rain shadow created by the Sierra Nevada mountain range to the West. To the East, it borders the Rocky Mountains. North, lie the Mojave Desert and the Sonoran Desert.

The Chihuahuan Desert stretches across most of the border between the United States and Mexico. The Colorado Plateau is a desertic region in the American states of Colorado, New Mexico, Utah and Arizona. It features scattered, isolated forests, and the Grand Canyon, shown earlier. It has the greatest concentration of National Parks Service units in the country.

Here again we break from the list to show the Sonoran Desert and the Mojave Desert together. While the Sonoran Desert covers part of the US-Mexico border, the Mojave lies north, within the US territory.

The Mojave Desert is a coastal desert, cut-off from humidity by the Tehachapi Mountains. The largest city in the Mojave is Las Vegas, Nevada.

Kyzyl Kum

The Kyzyl Kum is a desert in Central Asia, spanning the territories of Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan. Sand dunes cover most of its area, but there is land proper for agriculture around rivers and oases.

Taklamakan Desert

The Taklamakan Desert is an Asian desert surrounded by mountains from the south, west and north, and the Gobi Desert from the East. Sand dunes cover 85% of the Taklamakan and it spans parts of China, Pakistan and India. Also a pathway of the Silk Road, caravans would stop at oasis towns for relief.

Thar Desert

The Thar Desert or Great Indian Desert forms a natural boundary between India and Pakistan. Shifting sand dunes cover most of its area, with a handful of saltwater lakes. It covers the area where flowed the Sarasvati River, mentioned in an earlier chapter.

Atacama Desert

The Atacama Desert is the driest non-polar desert in the world. Stony terrain covers most of its area, with a presence of salt lakes and sand. The Chilean territory holds most of the desert’s core, but the surrounding transition zone includes parts of Peru, Bolivia and Argentina. The Chilean Coastal Range to the West and the Andes keep the Atacama in a permanent rain shadow. Because of low air humidity, inexistent cloud cover and high altitude, it is one of the best places in the world for astronomical observation.

Namib Desert

The Namib Desert is coastal desert in southern Africa, north of the Kalahari. From the East, the Drakensberg mountains keep moist clouds originated in the Indian Ocean from passing through. Humid air coming from the Atlantic Ocean, west of the desert, turn into fog by contact with warm dry winds coming from the East. As a result, the coastal area of the desert may experience up to 180 days of thick fog a year. Though a navigational hazard responsible for many sunk ships, the moisture is essential for desert life. As a result, the Namib is one of the driest deserts, and with the least precipitation. Most passing by rivers either dry up in the summer or vanish into the desert and there are no lakes. Some scientists believe it is the oldest desert in the world: between 55 and 80 million years old.

Dasht-e Kavir

The Dasht-e Kavir, or Great Salt Desert, is a desert in Iran. It receives little rain and snow each year, but there marshes, seasonal lakes and season river beds. Affected by extreme temperatures, they dry, leaving behind large crusts of salt. Human presence is sparse and limited to oases.

Dasht-e Lut

The Dasht-e Lut, or Lut Desert, is also a large salt desert in Iran, south-east of the Dasht-e Kavir. It is one of Earth’s driest and hottest places.

Forest types and distribution

Due to the connection between forests and biomes, here’s the biome world map again for reference:

A forest is a large area covered with trees or other woody vegetation. They make up for 26% of the world’s land area and contain 80% of the planet’s plant biomass. Different kinds of forest form in different parts of the world for a variety of reasons. In general, there are different kinds when nearer the poles, near the tropics and near the Equator. There are also variations according to altitude. Their size can vary with climate variations, introduction of foreign plant species and human influence. Natural fires and parasites may also contribute to shrinkage of forest area. Difficulty of human settlement or administrative decision might protect them from degradation. They can be set aside for hunting, logging, city climate control and tourism or general aesthetic enjoyment.

There are many proposed classifications for forests. We will use the one used by the The UN’s Environment Programme’s World Conservation Monitoring Centre. That leaves us at:

  • Temperate needleleaf;
  • Temperate broadleaf and mixed;
  • Tropical moist;
  • Sparse trees and parkland;
  • Forest plantations.

Let’s go through each of those giving examples.

Temperate Needleleaf

We know Temperate Needleleaf forests from the Taiga and the Montane Forest biomes. Coniferous trees are not only the dominant plant life, but in some cases the only present. We find Temperate Needleleaf forests in the Northern Hemisphere.

Temperate Broadleaf and mixed

We find Temperate Broadleaf and Mixed forests both in the North and the Southern Hemisphere. We know it from the “Temperate Broadleaf Forest” biome. In the Northern Hemisphere, these forests form as, moving further south, broadleaf trees become possible. Because needleleaf forests are also present, they earn the “mixed” epithet.

In the Southern Hemisphere, there are no Temperate Needleleaf forests. There, coniferous trees are always found accompanied by their broadleaf counterparts. Some of the countries with forests of this kind are: United States, China, Japan, Chile, Tasmania, Australia, New Zealand. They are also present in the Mediterranean region.

Tropical Moist

Tropical Moist includes the Subtropical and Tropical Rainforest biomes. It also includes flooded forests and swamps. In Brazil, two kinds draw special interest: várzea and cantão, on the course of large rivers in flat lands, flooded in the rainy season.

The "peat" in Peat Swamp comes from the accumulation of dead plant matter on the ground. This happens because the biomass production is greater than the rate of decomposition. The accumulation of peat is one of the largest carbon dumps in the planet.

Tropical Dry

Tropical and Subtropical Dry forests are forests in the tropics affected by seasonal drought. This includes the Monsoon, Tree Savanna and Subtropical Dry Forest biomes. In most of these forests, trees tend to lose their leaves before the dry season.

Sparse trees and parkland

Sparse trees and parkland are transition zones between forest and non-forested areas. One example is the area between tundras and taigas, where trees are present, but not in large numbers. As a result, tree canopy cover is between 10% and 30%. There is high presence of bryophytes, such as mosses.

Forest plantations

Forest plantations are man-made forests of rapid-growing timber trees for seasonal logging. Though poor in biodiversity, they are important, if for nothing else, to ease pressure for logging natural forests.

Diverging tectonic plates

If you search the map for diverging tectonic plates, you will see most diverging areas are under the sea, except for Iceland. The geology of Iceland is unique that way and, as a result, it contains more than 30 active volcanoes and geysers.

Now you may ask, what makes tectonic plates move? The answer is in this divergence area, something there is pushing the tectonic plates away. The name of the force responsible is "seafloor spreading". Unseen by most people and only recently discovered by scientists, it is one of the most important engines of the planet.

The solidification of the magma erupted from the exposed area between tectonic plates continually creates volcanoes and seafloor. As this process continues, new seafloor pushes away old seafloor and the tectonic plates themselves, as seen below.

Converging tectonic plates

When tectonic plates converge, either of two things can happen: one climbs over the other or both rise, pushing against each other. As a result, mountain chains form on the convergence zones over million of years.

A mountain is a tall landform, usually in the form of a peak, set apart from hills both by their height and by being steeper. Though some mountains stand isolated, most are part of mountain ranges. There are three types: volcanic, fold and block.

Volcanic Mountains

Volcanic mountains are or have been volcanoes, the result of one tectonic plate sliding over another. When this happens, the magma from under the rising plate comes out to the surface and solidifies, which forms the volcano. Now we see the main types of volcanoes.

Stratovolcanoes

Where magma eruptions are pre-hardened, they don’t spread over a large area, but instead gather in a cone shape, resulting in stratovolcanoes. They can also be called “composite volcanoes”, as different eruptions pile over each other.

Shield volcanoes

However, where the magma is more liquid, it tends to be expelled continually for long periods of time and to go further before solidification. The resulting volcano is spread out over hundreds of kilometers and looks like a shield lay flat on the floor.

Cinder cone volcanoes

Other eruption events are more sudden, erupting in an explosion of lava fountains and volcanic ash under pressure from volcanic gas. The material of the resulting volcano is less compact than found on the previous types, mostly loose rocks of solidified lava. They are cone-shaped and often have a dome on the peak.

Fold mountains

When two tectonic plates collide, the clashing section of the plates rise and become fold mountains. The material of fold mountains is hard rock, since that’s what we find under the surface. These mountains are still rising after earthquakes.

Block mountains

It is well known that there are faults between the planet’s tectonic plates, but there are faults inside them as well. The bedrock of a tectonic plate is not even, but is instead broken up into fault blocks, some as large as continents. Some fault blocks move vertically in relation to other neighboring fault blocks. Be it upwards or downwards movement, block mountains are the resulting height difference.

Introduction

Before we head on to the largest mountains in the world, we need to have a talk about measuring mountains.

First of all, the most common way to measure a mountain is by its height, meaning elevation above mean sea level. However, a list of peaks by height is boring. Since the Himalayas are so high compared to all other mountain chains, you have to go a long way down to find a mountain from any other region. There are other ways to measure height, some more meaningful and interesting for us.

There are many ways to measure mountains, more than a dozen. There's "isolation measure", "drop measure" and "spire measure", for example. We will follow for the most part "topographic prominence", which itself has many variances. There's "submarine prominence", "inverse prominence", "dominance" and "proportional prominence", for example.

Each of these have different results. In "The Finest Peaks: Prominence and other Mountain Measures", for example, Adam Helman describes "spire measure": “attempts to quantify the sense of impressiveness afforded a mountain due to great angularity”. If this seems interesting, there's a list of the top peaks according to the reduced spire measure in the related section.

So let’s take a moment to understand better the concept of topographic prominence. To start, please take a moment to understand the following image. The vertical lines show the prominence of the peaks.

Topographic prominence is often defined as “the height of a mountain above the saddle of the highest ridge connecting it to a peak higher still”. You may understand prominence as a measure of the independence of a mountain. The largest mountain in an area will score high, "taking the credit" for the whole region. But neighboring peaks will score lower, since they depend on the elevated area for their high elevation. As a result, a list of mountains by prominence correlates better with their cultural presence: they stand out the most.

That’s enough about this. Now we review a few interesting lists according to different measures, then move on to the mountains by prominence. After that, we will review mountains and volcanoes that are relevant for other reasons.

The Eight-Thousanders

The eight-thousanders are the 14 mountain on Earth taller than 8,000 m, all in the Himalayas. In addition to being a nice round cut-off number, the figure is also relevant to climbers.

The human body works best at sea-level. At an altitude of 2,100 m, we see the first signs of the effect of decreased atmospheric pressure on human blood. As you climb higher, pressure decreases exponentially, while temperature, only linearly. At the same time, montane climate itself becomes more extreme with height, due to the disturbing presence of the mountain itself. At 8,000 M high, the presence of oxygen in the air drops to one third of normal levels. Without supplementary oxygen, this causes a person to die in a few minutes. The body becomes weaker and the brain can’t function correctly, leading to bad decisions under stress. It was considered impossible to climb the Everest without supplementary oxygen, but Reinhold Messner disagreed.

Caves

There are several types of caves, but we will review only the most important. In general, a cave would be an area under the surface that is large enough for parts of it not to receive sunlight. Some are openings into rock, others are large areas under the ground big enough for a person to explore. Let's go through the types and see a few examples of each.

Cave Types

Shallow Caves

Let's start with a few types of small and shallow caves.

Rock Shelters

Rock shelters are useful as protection against the weather. They were often used by prehistoric men, making some important archaeological sites.

Grottos

Another name for shallow caves, grottos can be either manmade or natural. Since the apparition of Our Lady of Lourdes, in France, 1858, they have been used for Catholic shrines.

Sea caves

Sea caves are the result of wave erosion acting on coastal rock. The largest is Matainaka Cave, in New Zealand, which is 1.5 km long.

Solutional caves

Solutional caves are the most common, formed in soluble rocks over time, dissolved by the natural acidity in water. A few soluble rock types are: limestone (most common), chalk, dolomite, marble, salt and gypsum. As a result of the water seeping in from above, these caves feature speleothems, special formations of rock. The image below shows the most common speleothems.

The most famous are stalactites (on the ceiling) and stalagmites (on the floor). When connected, these form a column. The image below shows many more types of speleothems. Head on over to the Wikipedia article on speleothems to see it further explained.

Primary Caves

This classification applies to caves formed at the same time as the surrounding rock—that is, not by erosion. The most common are lava tubes, the result of a special way of lava solidification.

Corrasional or erosional caves

Formed by erosion, caused by water or wind. Some solutional caves start that way, then are flooded by a stream of water, which enlarges the hollow area.

Glacier caves

Formed in glaciers where ice has melted.

The climate

This is a crash course in basic Climatology, the science charged with studying the climate.

The distinction between climate and weather is fundamental. Climate refers to long term patterns, while weather is the climate condition in a short period—a day, for example.

The single most important factor to the climate is the Sun and how it doesn’t shine in the entire planet the same way. In the image below, you see that the Earth is tilted as it circles around the Sun. However it does also spin. As a result, there is a large region near the Equator that faces straight at the Sun. The regions far from the Equator face the Sun from an angle, so they get less sunlight.

The following image shows the intertropical convergence zone between the tropics: Cancer and Capricorn. From the tropics to the arctic and antarctic circles, we have the temperate zones. From the arctic and antarctic circles to the poles, we see the frigid zones. Insulation is different for each of these zones.

The Earth does not rotate around the Sun in a circle, but in an ellipses. Because of this, the Sun is closer in some parts of the year than in others. In the temperate zone, where sunlight is already lower, that is enough to cause the seasons. Around the Equator, always facing squarely at the Sun, the difference isn’t as great and there are no seasons. However, since the global climate is one entire system, it also faces changes: a rainy season and a dry season.

The atmosphere

On the image below, you will see the layers of the atmosphere. Only the troposphere and the stratosphere are of concern to us.

Most human activity, except for high-altitude aviation and spaceflight, happens in the troposphere. The stratosphere is relevant to us for two reasons: it is home to the jet stream and to the ozone layer.

Composition

Earth’s atmosphere consists roughly of:

  • 78% nitrogen;
  • 21% oxygen;
  • 1% other gases.

Both Nitrogen and Oxygen are abundant because they are heavy enough not to escape the atmosphere. Volcanic eruptions expelled the nitrogen gas and living organisms created the oxygen, over billions of years through photosynthesis. The 1% of other gases includes ozone, carbon dioxide, water and others. Both ozone and carbon dioxide are greenhouse gases, they help keep heat from the Sun in the planet surface. Without the greenhouse effect Earth would freeze, being unable to hold heat generated by the Sun. With a stronger greenhouse effect, the planet would become warmer. Photosynthesis, the origin of all the oxygen, uses carbon dioxide. In turn, it protects the same living organisms from genetic damage by ultraviolet from the Sun.

Atmospheric circulation

Alright, so it’s warmer in the Equator, it’s a little colder in the tropics and much colder in the poles. All that differently-heated air doesn’t just stay where it is. This is the most important knowledge you have to take out of this chapter. Everything that happens next in the climate follows from this difference in temperature: the result is "atmospheric circulation".

Atmospheric circulation is the movement of air throughout the atmosphere of the planet. It follows from the second law of thermodynamics, as the different sections of the planet exchange heat. Without the input of more heat from the Sun, this would result in entropy: the entire atmosphere would end with the same temperature. However, because the Sun heats the Equator more than the tropics and the poles every day, the process never stops.

Moving air is how atmospheric heat gets exchanged around: that's wind. The warm winds of the Equator blow to the tropics. The medium-warm winds of the temperate zone blow to the Equator and to the poles. The cold winds of the poles blow to the tropics or in a spiral around the poles, trapped. The result is what you see below.

So, we have one “cell” for each of the zones mentioned before. The Hadley Cell around the Equator, the Ferrel Cell between the tropics and the poles and the Polar Cell in the poles. On the image, the blue arrows are cold winds, the red arrows are warm winds.

Before we go into more detail, please notice the directions of surface wind. Around the Equator, surface wind blows West and toward the Equator. In the Ferrel Cell, surface wind blows East and toward the poles. In the poles, surface wind blows West and toward the tropics. The words in yellow are the names of each of those winds: trade winds, westerlies and polar easterlies. However, in reality winds don’t follow exactly that pattern because they function differently over land and sea. We will see a “real” wind map further below.

You may notice that as well as moving across the planet, winds also move vertically. This happens because cold air is heavier than hot air. As the hot air moves upward, however, it also cools down, becoming cold air. It then moves downward, as hot air takes its place. The Hadley Cell is so tall because warm winds meet in the Equator and there’s only one way from there: up.

There is one other wind movement we can observe. We have accounted for vertical movement and for movement across the planet. The latter doesn’t happen in a straight line though, and we haven’t accounted for that. The cold winds in the Hadley Cell move from east to west due to the Coriolis effect. As the Earth spins around its axis east to west, it drags the atmosphere, but not as much as the land. So a wind moving north while the Earth spins, when plotted on a stationary map, will seem to curve. Read that again, take a look at the map, look at the image below and try to wrap your mind around it.

Now that we have understood what traces the path of winds, let’s go through each of the three cells.

The Hadley Cell

Please take a moment to observe the Hadley Cell in the image from before.

As the sun heats the surface near the Equator, the air becomes hotter and rises, carrying humidity. It is then replaced by cold and dry air from the higher troposphere, which proceeds to warm up and gain humidity near the surface. Meanwhile, the hot and humid air forms rain clouds and, with altitude, becomes colder and rains down, losing humidity and mass. The now lost water in the air stored most of the heat, so the air becomes cold and dry again, so it falls back down to the surface. The end result is clear: heavy rainfall in the intertropical convergence zone, which the Hadley Cell covers. The word “Convergence” comes from the northeast and the southeast trade winds coming together.

The Polar Cell

Now let’s compare that to what happens in the poles. Because there isn’t as much heat from the Sun in the poles, the air is cold and dry. However, the air at the Arctic and Antarctic circles is still warmer than the air right on the poles, since it is in constant exchange with the warmer winds of the Ferrel Cell. This difference of temperature causes this warmer air to go toward the pole, where it rapidly cools off again. As a result, the poles function as a heat sink: whatever heated and humid air comes in contact with it, becomes cold and dry. Because it is so cold in the poles, the humidity does not fall as rain, but as snow. This sets the stage for the polar deserts we mentioned in the biomes chapter.

The Ferrel Cell

Now we circle back to analyze the Ferrel Cell, between the tropics and the arctic and antarctic circles. The Ferrel Cell receives both cold and dry air from the poles and warm and humid air from the intertropical convergence zone. The question is, which wins, that is, which is stronger? The answer is, it depends on the time of the year. When the Earth is closer to the Sun—during Summer—the warm winds of the Equator dominate. But when the Earth is further away from the Sun—during Winter— the cold winds of the poles expand and escape.

In the Ferrel Cell region, we find the westerlies winds.

Winds

Due to interactions between land and sea, the winds do not follow the general path outlined in the previous map. Below is the “real” wind map I mentioned before.

In blue, you have the westerlies. In yellow, the northeasterly trade winds. In orange, the southeasterly trade winds. The regions marked with the green and pink numbers are low-pressure areas. We will explain more about those later.

To understand the addition of land into this process, this is the most important fact: land warms faster than water, but also cools faster. As a result, in the warmer months land area will be warmer than the ocean. However, in the cold months the ocean keeps the heat it gets for longer, while land loses it faster. As a result, the ocean will be warmer than the land.

Northeast and Southeast trade winds

The northeast and southeast trade winds are cold surface winds moving from the Poles to the Equator. Both flow east to west due to the Coriolis Effect.

The Northeast trade winds allowed European expansion into the Americas. In a similar way, they carry hurricanes to the Caribbean and North America and cyclones to Asia.

Westerlies

The Westerlies, or anti-trades, blow in the opposite direction of the trade winds. During winter, when the polar winds are strongest, the Westerlies also increase in strength.

The Coriolis Effect

Now why do the Westerlies flow East, while the trade winds flow West? As we have seen before, without the Coriolis Effect winds would blow in a straight line, either North or South. Here’s the difference: the trade winds are escaping from the Equator, blowing to the poles. However, the Westerlies are escaping from the poles, blowing toward the Equator. Considering this, here is how it works due to the coriolis effect:

  • Northern Hemisphere:
  • Moving north to south: winds turn west (northern hemisphere trade winds);
  • Moving south to north: winds turn east (northern hemisphere westerlies);
  • Southern Hemisphere:
  • Moving north to south: winds turn east (southern hemisphere westerlies);
  • Moving south to north: winds turn west (southern hemisphere trade winds);

Monsoon

The Monsoon winds affects the region surrounding the Indian Ocean: West Africa, Southeast Asia and Australia. You may remember Monsoon Forests were one kind of biome we studied before. That biome shows one dry season and one wet season, when under the effect of monsoon rains, which the monsoon winds cause.

The different heating rates between land and water come into play to explain the monsoons. During summer, the Indian Ocean is colder than the land surrounding it. As a result, the air over land rises and is replaced by cool and wet air from the ocean. Upon reaching land, however, these winds are forced upward, which cools them further, releasing the humidity as rain. Those are the monsoon rains. This colder air then returns to the ocean, since it tends downward, like a waterfall from high land to low ocean.

This whole process happens in a similar manner to generate coastal winds all over the world, but it is more intense in the monsoon region. Slight differences happen throughout Asia, but this explanation is detailed enough for our purposes.

Jet stream

For all the talk about the interaction between land and water and its effect on winds patterns, there is one type that is not affected. Jet streams are so high up, between the troposphere and the stratosphere, that land and sea cover does not factor into its functioning.

Jet streams are fast and narrow air currents. They flow roughly between the three cells of atmospheric circulation: along the tropics and along the arctic and antarctic circles.

Their practical importance lies in shortening travel times for airplanes that fly high enough to meet them. They are important for the climate because they steer storms. For example, in both 2007 and 2012 intense storms hit Britain due to changes in the polar jet stream in the Northern Hemisphere.

Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the pressure exerted on everything on the surface by the weight of the Atmosphere. It is not easily apparent, but if you close an empty bottle on a mountain top, where the Atmosphere around you is rare and light, and then bring the bottle to the ocean level, the heavier atmosphere there will crush that bottle.

Other than mountaintops, we also find low atmospheric pressure on deserts. That’s because the air warm and moving upward, so it doesn’t exert much force downward.

High pressure areas are more important to us. Think back at the three atmospheric circulation cells, and how other than surface winds, they also had winds in the upper troposphere. Wherever you have cold winds coming down from two different atmospheric circulation cells in a same area, the pressure will be higher than in other places. In the same way, wherever you have warm wind going upward from two bordering cells, you have low pressure. Please take a look at the atmospheric circulation map with the green planet and find the blue H’s and the red L’s. Those are high and low atmospheric pressure regions.

So, what happens there? First of all, in low-pressure areas the winds are stronger, since warm air, because it is lighter, moves faster than cold air. It also rains, since the hot air moving upward carries humidity, which then rains down as the air cools.

Meanwhile, in high-pressure areas you have clear skies, as the air is dry. The lack of cloud cover also exposes the land to direct sunlight, but since there are no clouds to accumulate the heat, temperatures are lower.

All would be fine if it were not for the Coriolis Effect. Again, because the Earth is spinning, all this motion tends to happen in a circular pattern. This is the result:

Winds are stronger where the temperature difference that causes them to exist is larger. In low-atmospheric pressure areas, this difference can be very large and be created very fast because all that rising, wet and warm air cools off fast. As long as there’s warm water to keep the process going, it doesn’t stop. That’s why hurricanes damage coastal cities, but doesn’t go much farther. Once the hurricane is over land, it lacks fuel—the warm and wet winds—to keep it going.

Ocean circulation

So all that happens in the atmosphere, but what about the oceans? After all, ocean heat is also uneven based on distance to the Equator. In a similar way to Atmospheric Circulation, we have Ocean Circulation, resulting in the Thermohaline circulation, or the “global conveyor belt”.

They are important for the weather in many ways, but especially because warm currents help warm up surface winds.

Global Conveyor Belt

As a result of Ocean circulation, ocean currents follow the dynamics pictured below.

Again, the red and blue lines mean warm and cold currents, this time with a twist. Because only the surface of water heats up, deep currents are cold, while surface currents are warm. You can see two areas near the poles labeled “deep water formation”, where surface currents cool off and move deeper underwater. You may also notice that in two areas the cold currents warm up and rise to the surface: the eastern coast of Africa and the Pacific Ocean.

Possibly the most striking feature of the image is the circle around Antarctica, with warm currents on the outside and cold currents on the inside. That’s the Antarctic Circumpolar current, part of the previously mentioned perpetual storm raging around Antarctica.

Something you might not notice at a glance is that, on the Northern Hemisphere, currents run clockwise while, on the Southern Hemisphere, they flow counter-clockwise. Just as before, that’s a result of the Coriolis Effect.

Knowledge of ocean currents was essential up to the steam-era and is still important to lower transoceanic travel costs.

Gulf Stream

In the global conveyor map, take a moment to find the warm current going from Antarctica through the Atlantic Ocean to the North Pole. In the ocean currents world map, you will see it has been broken up into the following currents:

  • Benguela;
  • South Equatorial;
  • North Equatorial;
  • Caribbean;
  • Loop;
  • Gulf Stream;
  • North Atlantic;

Of these, the Gulf Stream is of special importance. It warms up the eastern coast of North America and the western coast of Europe by carrying warm currents from the Equator.

Europeans have known about it since the 16th century, and Spanish ships used it to travel from the Caribbean back to the homeland. In the 18th century, it was further studied by the American Benjamin Franklin, leading to significant gains in travel.

The current shape of the Gulf Stream—and thus of the entire global conveyor belt—developed from the formation of the Isthmus of Panama, 12 to 15 million years ago. Before then, ocean currents would flow from the Atlantic to the Pacific and both Eastern North America and Western Europe were colder. This makes the creation of the Isthmus of Panama one of the most important geological events on Earth.

Humboldt Current

The Humboldt Current is named Peru/Chile in the ocean currents world map. It consists of cold currents from the South Pole, flowing north along the south american west coast. As a result, it makes Chile and the Peruvian coast colder. The Humboldt Current is home to one of the richest marine life ecosystems in the world.

The end-result

Below you will find a climate world map based on the Köppen climate classifications. It somewhat correlates with the biomes world map we have seen before, but let’s examine it from the perspective of the climate.

Ice cap, highlands, non-permanent ice, tundra and the subarctic

In Greenland and Antarctica, we have “Ice cap”, meaning that those areas are permanently covered in ice. There’s an important distinction to be made here. “Ice sheets” or “continental glaciers” are ice covers with an area greater than 50,000Km2. There are two of them in the planet: on Greenland and on Antarctica. We call “ice caps” anything smaller, from mountaintop glaciers to the frozen Arctic. The ice cap climate region, which is not the same as an ice cap in general, covers both ice caps and ice sheets.

This is confusing because we find ice caps on the “highlands” climate region. These are some of the tallest mountain chains we studied before, you should be able to identify them.

In addition to this, and keeping with the "ice" theme, we have the non-permanent ice areas. This is a sort of alternate to the "ice cap" classification.

The subarctic and tundra regions are cold due to proximity to the Arctic. As we have seen, they are covered by the tundra and taiga biomes.

Humid continental and Humid subtropical

Humid continental areas feature the classic four seasons system, shielded from a greater influence of rain-carrying winds by their inner placing.

In contrast, Humid subtropical areas are typically on eastern coasts, receiving rainfall from westerlies winds.

Marine west coast and Mediterranean

Ever heard that in London or Seattle it rains a lot? That’s the marine west coast climate, constantly affected by rain-carrying westerlies winds (which, you must remember, travel east to west). These are the same rains affected by rainshadow, and you can see the resulting deserts in the map.

The Mediterranean climate, just like the biome, shows hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters.

Arid and Semiarid

We are well familiarized with this condition from the biomes chapter. These areas receive very low amounts of rain.

Tropical wet and Tropical wet and dry

We also know both of these from their respective biomes. In tropical wet areas we see tropical rainforests. In tropical wet and dry, tropical and subtropical dry forests. We distinguish them by the amount of rain they receive.
